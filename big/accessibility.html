The Curb Cut Effect & Web Accessibility

If you have turned on closed captioning in a noisy room, used a video transcript to study, or even just written an email, then you have benefitted from assistive technology. Shadi Abou-Zahara, Accessibility Strategy and Technology Specialist at the W3C, describes digital accessibility as “the electronic version of a curb cut or ramp” (Bateman). This clear comparison links the concepts of Universal Design with web design and development. Having a conversation about accessibility is vital, especially at the beginning of the design and development stage of a website or web tool project, not only because it may expand the pool of potential users or minimize legal risks, but it primarily addresses a basic human right (UN CRPD)

Getting started with web accessiblity does not have to be complicated or confusing process. The W3C WAI provides a clear first stop for designers and developers at any level, providing comprehensive introductory tutorials on topics such as keyboard access and multimedia alternatives, to evaluation examples and ways to address interim repairs if accessibility issues need to be resolved immediately before a full overhaul can be done. It’s worth acknowledging that shifting one’s perspective away from viewing accessibility as a challenge, cost factor, or potential legal issue, will allow these practices to actually drive more elegant and streamlined solutions. To quote Ida Aalen, co-founder & CPO of Confrere.com, “If it’s complicated to make something accessible, that’s often a sign that the design or idea itself is just too complicated. (Aalen)”

Norway provides a clear example of how legislation that requires both public and private sector websites to meet WCAG 2.0 standards can be passed and widely embraced. Mandatory implementation changed how clients, designers and developers view integrating assistive techniques, “...because of the law, accessibility can’t be an afterthought. Accessibility is intertwined with all aspects of web design, and is dependent on team effort...You can have aesthetics and accessibility at the same time, it’s just a matter of focus. (Aalen)”

When developers and designers take the initiative to employ internationally recognized standards as laid out by the W3C in WCAG 2.0 and 2.1 they are actually reducing potential costs and future efforts. “If you create a system where people with disabilities are shut out from doing...very basic things, you are not only lessening their quality of life, you’re also likely to increase costs for both society and private companies. If people can’t do it online, they’ll have to call your company, or send a physical letter, or even show up at your office. That’s not cost-effective.(Aalen)” By integrating accessibility practices and continuing to innovate past the status quo, developers and designers can contribute to the normalization of such practices and to destigmatizing assistive technologies - doing so will continue to improve Web usability for everyone. (Disability Science Review)
